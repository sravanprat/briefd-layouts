Homepage Layout Options

3 approaches for executive content consumption · CIO/CTO review

The Problem

Executives spend significant time switching between sources to stay current. A CIO preparing for a board meeting shouldn't have to stitch together information from multiple tabs and tools. Briefd's home screen is the single surface that replaces that switching — delivering briefs, meeting materials, podcasts, recent news, and infographics in one view, prioritized by what's relevant this week.

Why These 3 Layouts

Each layout reflects a different mode a CIO uses — ambient awareness, deep-dive prep, or scheduled catch-up. We're testing which mode maps most naturally to how CIOs actually open the app.

Option 1

The Newsroom

Inspired by Bloomberg and financial desks — many signals visible simultaneously, editorial hierarchy drives priority.

Best fit: Morning scan, ambient awareness

Option 2

The Briefing Room

Mirrors how senior staff prepare principals — one deep topic, full context rail. Like a Notion doc with live data attached.

Best fit: Pre-meeting or pre-board prep

Option 3

The Feed

Familiar scroll-and-scan from tools execs already use daily (LinkedIn, Feedly). Lowest adoption friction.

Best fit: Commute, scheduled daily catch-up

Layout Evaluation

Layout 1

Newsroom — Multi-panel dashboard

When to use: opening the app for a quick morning scan

Hero story left, live KPI ticker center, podcast + AI chat right. Everything visible at once — no navigation required to assess the day's landscape. Modeled on how a trading desk or newsroom editor monitors signals.

Strengths

  • Maximum signal at a glance — no clicks to read the landscape
  • Ticker creates time-awareness without separate notifications
  • Strongest visual differentiation — nothing else looks like this

Watch

  • Highest cognitive load — takes time to learn to read efficiently
  • Fixed panel structure limits flexibility as content types grow
  • Breaks on smaller screens and laptops — desktop only

Layout 2

Briefing Room — Three-column document

When to use: 20 min before a board meeting or policy decision

Topic nav left, full executive brief center, companion rail right (podcast, KPIs, charts, news, meeting docs, chat). Everything about one topic, in one screen. No tab switching, no assembly required.

Strengths

  • Best for decision-prep — full context without switching tabs
  • Companion rail keeps sources and chat visible alongside the brief
  • Clear hierarchy — center brief anchors attention, rail supports

Watch

  • Assumes the user enters with a specific topic — weak for browsing
  • Requires wide screen (1200px+) — not suited to mobile or tablet
  • Right rail gets crowded as content types expand over time

Layout 3

Feed — Scrollable cards + sticky sidebar

When to use: daily commute, scheduled 10-min catch-up

Vertical card feed (brief, podcast, infographic, news, meeting prep) with topics and AI chat pinned right. Same muscle memory as LinkedIn or Feedly — but filtered to what matters for a CIO's role.

Strengths

  • Zero learning curve — familiar pattern from daily tools
  • Most extensible — new content types slot in without redesigning
  • Works across screen sizes including mobile and tablet

Watch

  • Least differentiated visually — risks looking like another news app
  • No fixed hierarchy — priority depends entirely on card ordering logic
  • Scroll fatigue risk if volume grows without strong curation

Recommendation

Our view

Start with Layout 3 as the default, with Layout 2 as the desktop prep mode. Layout 3 works across all devices — phone, tablet, desktop — making it the right foundation for daily habit use. Layout 2 is purpose-built for deep prep at a desk before a board meeting or policy decision, where a wide screen is available and the user needs full context in one view. The two are not mutually exclusive: L3 is the home screen, L2 activates when a meeting is on the calendar and the user is on desktop.

Layout 1 has the strongest visual identity but the highest cognitive load and is desktop-only. Best reserved as a power-user mode.

Suggested next step: Walk 2–3 target CIOs through both layouts in context — L3 on a phone during a commute scenario, L2 on a laptop before a meeting. Observation beats preference surveys.
